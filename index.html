Vision

Foundation models are advancing rapidly, and robots can now recognize objects and understand commands. But recognition is not execution. For a robot to reliably act in the real world, it needs deep understanding of dynamics, control theory, and reinforcement learning. The gap between "seeing" and "doing" is where the real challenge lies.

This is true across every platform—drones, quadrupeds, humanoids, and task-specific machines each bring their own physical constraints. I'm taking my first steps toward that challenge, and beginning to explore how diverse robots could be operated under a unified system.

Read more about my vision and projects on my About Me page.

Why I Aim to Be a Generalist

What drew me to reinforcement learning was its elegance: learning optimal behaviors through trial and error. But I realized that simulations alone aren't enough—to make robotics meaningful, I needed to build real robots. This meant diving into soldering, electrical engineering, and mechanical design.

This journey taught me that technologies are tools for solving problems, not ends in themselves. What matters isn't mastering a specific algorithm—it's understanding the problem deeply and approaching it from first principles. Rather than falling into "tutorial hell," I've embraced learning through building and failing.
