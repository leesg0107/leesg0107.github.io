Every aircraft has landing gear. It deploys for takeoff and landing, and the rest of the flight? It just sits there — dead weight. Commercial planes retract theirs to reduce drag (up to 50% reduction, according to early NACA research from the 1930s). But small drones can't retract their landing gear — they're too simple and lightweight for that mechanism.
 
So I asked a question: **what if the landing gear could do something useful while the drone is in the air?**
 
Specifically — what if the structural struts that keep the drone upright on the ground could double as a gripper? No extra robotic arm, no added payload. Just the landing gear itself, repurposed.
 
That's TALON: a quadrotor that uses its own landing gear as a parallel gripper to autonomously pick up and transport objects.
 
**GitHub**: [github.com/leesg0107/TALON](https://github.com/leesg0107/TALON)

## This Isn't a New Idea — But No One's Made It Autonomous
 
I should be upfront: people have explored dual-purpose landing gear before.
 
- **Wang et al. (2025)** designed a rigid landing gear with a spring-driven quick-release mechanism that closes in under 0.2 seconds. Impressive mechanical design — but grasping is triggered manually. No autonomous flight control.
- **Cheung et al. (2024)** made a pneumatic soft gripper that doubles as landing gear when deflated. It can grasp objects in flight, but the control architecture handles only gripper actuation — approach, docking, and transport aren't integrated.
- There's a whole lineage of **bird-inspired perching systems** (Stanford's SNAG, various tendon-driven grippers) where the drone's weight passively closes a claw. These are elegant, but they're designed for perching on branches, not picking up free-standing objects from a surface.
What none of these systems do is the full pipeline: **fly to the object → position yourself precisely above it → descend to dock the landing gear around it → close → lift → fly somewhere else → release**. That end-to-end autonomy is what TALON attempts.

## The Geometry That Makes This Hard
 
Before diving into how the system works, let's look at what makes the docking phase uniquely challenging.
 
TALON's landing gear has two rigid struts spaced 100 mm apart. The target object is an 80 mm cube. That leaves **10 mm of clearance per side**. One centimeter.
 
```
        ┌── Strut ──┐     ┌── Strut ──┐
        │            │     │            │
        │   10mm ┌───┴─────┴───┐ 10mm  │
        │   gap  │   80mm box  │  gap   │
        │        └─────────────┘        │
        │         100mm total           │
```
 
For comparison, most aerial grasping research uses soft or compliant grippers that offer generous tolerance for positioning errors. Fishman et al. (2021) replaced a quadrotor's landing gear with silicone fingers that passively conform around objects; Appius et al. (2022) used Fin Ray soft fingers that deform on contact. TALON's rigid plates offer no such forgiveness. If the drone is off by more than 10 mm in the X-axis, the strut hits the box instead of sliding around it.
 
The Y-axis (plate direction) is more relaxed — 64 mm of clearance per side. This asymmetry matters: the controller has to be much more precise in X than in Y. It's the single most important design constraint in the project.

## How the Mission Works — Four Phases
 
I decomposed the full mission into four phases, each handled by a different controller. Here's the state machine:
 
```
[APPROACH]  ──→  [DOCK]  ──→  [CLIMB]  ──→  [DELIVERY]
    RL           Analytical      Analytical       RL
  Stage 1       PD controller   PD (low-gain)   Stage 4
```
 
Why not use one controller for everything? Because each phase has fundamentally different requirements. I'll walk through each.
 
### Phase 1: Approach (RL)
 
The drone starts at a random position 2–3 m away from the target, at 3 m altitude. It needs to navigate to a point directly above the target object.
 
This phase uses a **reinforcement learning policy** (PPO, trained in Isaac Lab with 4,096 parallel environments). The RL policy outputs body-frame accelerations and yaw rate at 150 Hz.
 
Why RL here? Because the approach involves:
- Random starting positions (no fixed trajectory to follow)
- Wind disturbances (0.5 N amplitude, randomized)
- Mass uncertainty (±10% domain randomization)
RL handles this variability naturally — it learns a robust policy that generalizes across conditions. The policy follows 3–4 intermediate waypoints with randomized lateral offsets before converging on a hover point 0.5 m above the target.
 
Accuracy requirement: get within ~30 cm of the target. That's it. The hard part comes next.
 
### Phase 2: Dock (Analytical Controller)
 
This is the core of the project and the hardest phase.
 
The drone must descend from 50 cm above the box, thread the landing gear around the box with 10 mm tolerance, and maintain contact with a physically reactive object long enough to secure a grasp. The box is free-standing on a pedestal — it moves when touched.
 
This phase uses an **analytical controller with gain scheduling** — not RL. I'll explain why in a future post (short version: I tried RL first, it failed at 12.7% success rate, and the failure analysis is one of the most interesting parts of this project).
 
The analytical controller does three things simultaneously:
 
**XY alignment**: Asymmetric PD control tracks the box position. X-axis gains are higher than Y-axis gains because X clearance is tighter. When the gripper gets within 4 cm of the box and 3 cm XY, the gains shift — Kp drops, Kd rises — creating a compliance-like mode that avoids pushing the box.
 
**Gated descent**: The drone only descends when XY alignment is good enough. The standoff altitude is a continuous function of XY error:
- XY error > 15 cm → hold at 30 cm above box (fix alignment first)
- XY error ~ 5 cm → descend to 12 cm (coarse alignment achieved)
- XY error < 2 cm → descend to 2 cm (final docking)
If wind pushes the drone off-center mid-descent, a hysteresis mechanism prevents unnecessary abort — small deviations are tolerated, only major displacement (>25 cm) triggers a pullback.
 
**Contact handling**: Once the box is inside the gripper envelope, the controller holds position for about 1 second, then closes the plates. After closure, it lifts 3 cm to physically verify the box is gripped — if the box stays on the pedestal, the grasp is classified as failed and the system retries. The full sequence from first containment to climb takes about 2.2 seconds.
 
There's also a **stuck recovery** mechanism: if the gripper catches on a box edge and stalls for 0.67 seconds, the drone automatically pulls up 30 cm and retries the descent.
 
### Phase 3: Climb (Analytical Controller, Low Gains)
 
After a successful grasp, the drone ascends to cruising altitude with the payload. This is separated from the Dock phase because the control requirements are opposite — Dock needs aggressive precision tracking, Climb needs gentle acceleration to avoid shaking the payload loose.
 
The same analytical controller runs here, but with **reduced gains**. High gains during climb cause rapid acceleration that oscillates the payload and risks dislodging it from the gripper. Lower gains produce a smooth, gentle ascent at ~0.3 m/s.
 
A **fake grasp detector** also runs during climb: if the drone has ascended 25 cm but the box is still at pedestal height, the gripper closed on empty air. The system descends and retries.
 
### Phase 4: Delivery (RL)
 
The drone transports the payload to a delivery location 2–3 m away at 2.5 m altitude.
 
This uses a separate RL policy (Stage 4) trained specifically for loaded flight. The key differences from Stage 1:
- Observation space includes a **payload mass estimate** (23D vs. 22D)
- Tighter tilt constraints (26° vs. 30°) — because aggressive tilting shifts the payload's center of mass and risks detachment
- An **overshoot penalty** that slows the drone near waypoints to prevent U-turns that could shake the payload loose
The Stage 4 policy is trained on thousands of pre-simulated grasp states — successful dock+climb outcomes collected from the analytical controller — so it always starts from a realistic loaded-flight configuration.

## The Control Hierarchy Underneath
 
All four phases share the same inner-loop controller. Every outer-loop command — whether from an RL policy or the analytical controller — is expressed as a body-frame acceleration vector and a yaw reference. The inner loop runs at 300 Hz and converts this to motor thrusts through:
 
1. Desired attitude computation (differential flatness)
2. SO(3) geometric attitude controller (Lee et al., 2010)
3. Motor allocation and first-order dynamics (τ = 0.02 s)
This means swapping between RL and analytical control is just swapping the source of the acceleration command. No low-level changes needed. It's what makes the whole pipeline modular.

## Current Results
 
| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Standalone docking (analytical controller) | ~70% |
| End-to-end mission success | ~68% |
| RL-only docking (for comparison) | 12.7% |
 
The system runs entirely in simulation (Isaac Lab on an RTX 4090). Sim-to-real transfer is future work.
